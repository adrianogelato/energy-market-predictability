Energy market predictability

Forecasting the German day-ahead power market, and measuring where forecasts fail. Real market data, explicit baselines, statistics that are allowed to say "no effect".

Three questions drove this project: can you predict tomorrow's cheapest electricity hours, what is that prediction worth in euros, and can a scheduled mega-event (the 2026 World Cup) move the market? The answers, computed live from the latest pipeline run:

1  Smart timing is worth real money. The forecasting model is not.

Shifting a daily EV charge into the cheapest hours saves …/year versus charging anytime (summer-rate annualization). But a trivial lookup table, "charge at the usual cheap times", captures … of that value; the weather-driven model adds …/year against it.

Product implication: a smart-charging feature on this market needs automation and UX, not machine learning. The cheap hours are that stable, in summer. Whether this holds in winter is an open, testable question.

Built as a portfolio project for product management / product ownership roles in the energy domain. The pipeline: aWATTar and ENTSO-E data, a walk-forward backtest against naive baselines, matched comparable-day event studies with placebo tests, published as a static page a GitHub Actions job refreshes daily. Every design decision is argued in the README.
