Does the World Cup move electricity prices?

Testing whether prices during match hours differ from weather-comparable days. Germany, 2026 tournament window.

The answer so far: no — not detectably.

Once weather and season are controlled for, prices during match hours look like prices on ordinary days, and actual demand did not measurably deviate from what the grid had forecast. One subset (overnight kickoffs) looked suggestive on the early interim data, but it faded as the window filled in, and a placebo test shows a blip that size appears by chance in random data too. The numbers below are the evidence.

How to read the numbers on this page (30 seconds)

Effect: how much higher or lower the price (or demand) was during match hours than on comparable days. The headline quantity.

t-statistic: signal-to-noise. Roughly, |t| above 2 hints at a real effect; below 2 the data is consistent with "nothing happened". Here the t's are additionally optimistic because match days share control days, which is why the placebo test matters more.

Placebo (permutation) p: the strictest check. Rerun the whole analysis on randomly chosen "match days" thousands of times; p is the share of random runs that look at least as extreme as the real one. p=0.40 means: 40% of pure-chance runs look this impressive, so the real one proves nothing. "Family-wise" corrects for having examined several subsets and reporting the best-looking one.

Minimum detectable effect (MDE): what the study could have seen. A null result only rules out effects larger than this, so every "no effect" on this page really means "no effect above the MDE".

Within-day diff-in-diff: a second estimate that compares match hours against the same day's other hours, which cancels out the season slowly shifting under the whole window. If it disagrees with the main estimate, the "effect" was drift, not the event.

On this page

    The question and the method

    People watching a match shift when they cook, heat, and run appliances, which changes demand. But price is mostly driven by weather (sun and wind), so a fair test has to hold weather constant.

    One timing subtlety: the day-ahead price is set in an auction at 12:00 the day before delivery. Nothing that happens during a match can move that day's day-ahead price, so this price test measures whether traders anticipated a match effect, not whether the match caused one. The forecast-error test further down is the one that can catch a real-time surprise.

    For each match day, the analysis finds the five most weather-comparable non-match days and compares the price during that day's match hours against the same clock hours on those comparable days. The average difference is the estimated match effect. "Comparable" is defined in one place (matching.py): daily temperature mean and max, a solar proxy (radiation when available, otherwise cloud cover), and wind when available, all z-scored; controls must share the day type (weekday / Saturday / Sunday-or-holiday, so a public holiday never stands in for a normal weekday).     The more direct test: did demand beat the forecast?

    Prices only tell you whether the market anticipated an effect. The cleaner measure is the day-ahead load forecast error (actual demand minus what was forecast). A positive spike during match hours means real demand ran above the forecast, which is the market being surprised.

    Honest reading: this is an observational estimate, not proof of causation. Match scheduling correlates with weekends and holidays, which have their own demand patterns; the weekday-type control reduces but does not remove that. A result near zero is a real finding, not a failure.
