How advanced does a price forecaster need to be?

Six models, from a lookup table to gradient boosting, one full backtested year, identical scoring. The value-of-complexity study.

The answer: a lookup table.

Over … backtested days, a 28-day rolling lookup table ("average price per hour over the last four weeks, pick the cheapest three") saves …/year for a household shifting a daily 11 kWh EV charge — … of what perfect foresight would earn (…/year). No model on the ladder beats it overall, including gradient boosting fed perfect weather.

The reasoning, in seven steps

  1. The action is picking hours, not predicting prices. A smart charger must choose 3 of 24 hours; it is judged by what those hours cost at real prices.
  2. There is a hard ceiling. Picking the true cheapest hours every day earns a fixed amount set by the price spread itself. No algorithm can exceed it.
  3. The test is fair. Every strategy is backtested walk-forward: to predict a day, it may only use data from before that day.
  4. The dumbest informed strategy nearly maxes out. The 28-day lookup table captures ~93% of the ceiling.
  5. Why: German cheap hours are structurally pinned. Solar floods midday, demand dies overnight. Weather changes how cheap those hours are, rarely which hours they are. Models predict price levels, spending their capacity on variance the hour-picking task never rewards — and their level errors occasionally reshuffle the ranking, making them actively worse (see the residual chart below).
  6. The winter nuance. Heating and scarce daylight make the winter shape genuinely variable, so models do win there — by about €1 per winter for this household. Direction confirmed, economics irrelevant.
  7. The product conclusion. Algorithm choice moves a few euros a year; automated timing at all moves about two hundred. Engineering budget belongs in automation, onboarding and trust, not the forecaster — at household scale.

The critical questions are design decisions

"Maybe the models were just badly built?"
The ceiling caps the argument: perfect foresight earns the maximum, the lookup table takes ~93% of it, so any model — however brilliant — competes for the remaining few euros a year. The upside is bounded before the model quality debate starts. (And gradient boosting with perfect weather inputs is a strong baseline; it lost.)
"Feeding models actual weather flatters them, no?"
Yes, deliberately — and they still lost. Real weather forecasts would make every weather-using rung worse, while the lookup table needs no forecast at all. The conclusion is conservative by construction.
"Is linear regression really machine learning?"
Textbook yes, industry arguably. Irrelevant here: the ladder includes gradient boosting and k-nearest-neighbour kernel regression, unambiguously ML.
"Why a 28-day lookup window?"
Rolling, so a winter day is judged against winter. A full-history average would have been a strawman for the models to beat.
"Does this verdict hold at scale?"
No — and that boundary is part of the finding. An aggregator trading GWh prices 0.4 ct/kWh of regret very differently. This is a household-product verdict.
"Only one year?"
One weather year, hourly resolution. Known open items, documented in the ROADMAP: scoring against archived weather forecasts, 15-minute products, multi-year windows.
